## The Hidden Cost of Acceptable Medical Device Field Failure Rates

With sustainability being a big talking point in the MedTech
world right now its important that devices that are failing are not just
accepted as 'the norm' or 'just the cost of doing business'. If a Field Service
Engineer (FSE) is going onsite to fix a problem, then you need to understand
why that problem occurred. 

It could simply be premature failure, or a simple user error
which has caused the device to fail in which case you probably don't need to do
anything about it. However, if you have multiple FSE's visiting multiple
customers and they are all seeing the same part failing, that's not something
you can ignore. That is a design issue that needs to be investigated. 

Sending your FSE's out to the same problem repeatedly will
bleed your margins. Where as if you carry out proper Root Cause Analysis (RCA) then you are much more likely to highlight a design failure or a component that is not right for the task at hand. This information can then be fed back into the R&D cycle to be fixed on the V2.0 of the device. It also allows you to implement a field process to fix the problem on all affected devices.

## Moving Beyond the Surface: Effective RCA for Medical Equipment

True Root Cause Analysis (RCA) is more than just a box ticking exercise. It is about the device architecture, the environment, the users and all other possible factors. A proper RCA will investigate all possibilities that lead to the component failure. Comparing a faulty unit with a functioning one. Replicating the situation where it failed. Understanding the bigger picture is just as important as understanding the symptom.\
\
For example I once worked on a device where it had become 'the norm' to replace at least one USB port on every PPM visit. All FSE's would carry a large stock of them in their tool kits as it was just expected to need them. The broken USB port was the symptom of a clearly much larger problem with the device. 

During design it was decided that the USB port would be placed around hip height on the side of the device. This meant that if the users left a flash drive plugged in then it would inevitably get caught as someone walked past and the USB drive would be broken.

The USB port was moved on the next design version of the device. Unfortunately the USB port could not be moved on existing devices, so I suspect even today FSEs working on those older systems are still carrying spare USB ports with them. Those USB ports are £15 on RS Components and it was at least one or two used per day. That soon adds up.\
\
Another example proving it is usually a design flaw rather than a component failure, is that a system was designed on a lab bench with no thoughts on how it would behave in the field. A ribbon cable in the back of the LCD Screen had a ferrite magnet around it to counter interference. However the weight of the magnet was not supported. So it worked perfectly when sat on a bench. However once the device was shipped, it would bounce around in a delivery van or on an aircraft pallet. By the time it arrived onsite the magnet had pulled the cable out of the LCD, meaning an FSE needed to remove the covers and plug it back in. The fix for this was to remove the magnet from all future devices and the issue went away.

## Engineering the Permanent Fix and Reducing Medical Device Downtime

From the examples given above it is clear that having proper RCA is essential. The information gathered gets fed back into the R&D loop, allowing design changes where there are required. 

The USB port being moved means that it no longed gets broken and isn't just accepted as an ongoing cost. Removing the ferrite magnet meant that an FSE did not need to open the case on an install, and after some other service steps were implemented, that device went to a customer self installation saving £1500+ per install in FSE costs alone.\

Field Service Engineers are a massive operational overhead. Stop bleeding margin by sending them to swap the same components due to a broken Root Cause Analysis process.
